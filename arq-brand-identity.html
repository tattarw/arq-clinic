01   Brand Strategy

The real
problem.

India's entire medical system is built on a single assumption: the doctor knows best. The patient arrives with symptoms. The doctor decides the solution. The pharmacist enforces the gate.


But a new kind of person has emerged. Someone who has done the research. Who listens to Huberman. Who knows the difference between modafinil and armodafinil. Who has already decided what they need. And yet — they still have to perform illness to get it.


The pharmacist's raised eyebrow. The GP who won't prescribe what you've asked for. The guilt of mixing a prescription item into an OTC order, hoping nobody notices. The feeling of being treated like you're trying to get away with something — when actually, you just know what you want.


That feeling is the problem Arq solves. Not access. Not convenience. The feeling.

"You shouldn't have to perform illness
to get what you already know you need."

The opportunity isn't to build a better pharmacy. It's to build the first healthcare brand that respects the informed patient — that treats knowing what you want as a credential, not a threat.

02   The Customer

Meet
Karan.

He's 31. Works in product or finance. Mumbai or Bangalore. He has opinions about sleep architecture and tracks his HRV on a WHOOP. He's read the Attia newsletter. He takes creatine and magnesium glycinate and knows exactly why.


He's been googling "buy armodafinil India" for six months. He knows the chemist near his office stocks it, but the last time he tried, the pharmacist asked for a prescription in a way that made him feel like a criminal. So he stopped trying.


He doesn't want a doctor's opinion. He wants a doctor's signature. There's a difference, and nobody in India has ever acknowledged it.

What he listens to
Huberman Lab. Peter Attia. Lex Fridman. Podcasts where smart people talk about optimising the body like a system, not managing it like a problem.
What he spends on
WHOOP. Creatine. AG1. Premium things that work. He doesn't hunt for the cheapest option — he hunts for the right one.
What he's afraid of
Being treated like a patient. Being told he needs to justify what he wants. Being made to feel like he's doing something wrong when he's actually doing the homework.
What he can't admit
He's been ordering modafinil from grey market sites. He's not proud of it. He'd pay more for something clean, legal, and delivered without theatre.
What he wants
To be treated like an adult. A system that says: you've done the research, we'll handle the paperwork.
What he tells friends
"You have to try this." When he finds something that actually works, he becomes a missionary. Word of mouth is his media channel.

Karan is also Priya — 29, works in consulting, PCOS diagnosis she manages herself because no gynecologist has ever given her a satisfying answer. She wants spironolactone and tretinoin and doesn't want to explain herself to anyone. She'll find Arq through Karan. And she'll stay longer.

03   The Brand Idea

Your body.
Your call.

Four words. Everything flows from them.


"Your body" — ownership. Not your doctor's body. Not the pharmacist's call to make. Yours. This is a radical statement in the context of Indian healthcare, where the system is built on the premise that the professional knows best.


"Your call" — authority. And a deliberate double meaning. The call you make as the decision-maker. And the doctor's call that comes after you buy — the 5-minute conversation that turns your decision into a legal prescription. The process is encoded in the tagline itself.

"Your body. Your call." isn't a wellness slogan.
It's a transfer of power.

What makes this brand idea durable is that it works at every level simultaneously:


Functionally — it describes exactly what Arq does. You decide. The doctor calls. It ships.


Emotionally — it restores the dignity that the pharmacist's raised eyebrow took away. You're not asking permission. You're exercising a right.


Culturally — in a country where healthcare is built on deference, this is genuinely radical. Not dangerous radical. Quietly, confidently radical.

04   Brand Pillars

What Arq
stands for.

Informed
not naive
Arq's customer has done the research. Arq meets them there — never explains what they already know, never talks down, never adds unnecessary medical theatre. The brand assumes intelligence. It uses the right terminology without condescension. It shares the science without the sermon.
Unapologetic
not reckless
Wanting semaglutide isn't a confession. Wanting modafinil isn't shameful. Wanting THC:CBD isn't suspicious. Arq never frames desire as a problem to be solved. There is no "struggling with" in Arq's vocabulary. You want something. Arq gets it for you. That's the entire interaction.
Rigorous
not paranoid
The doctor call isn't theatre. Blood tests for TRT aren't bureaucracy. Arq is medically serious because its customers are medically serious. Clinical rigour is a service, not a hurdle. The brand's credibility depends on being genuinely careful — which makes the freedom it offers feel earned, not reckless.
Discreet
not secretive
Plain packaging isn't about hiding. It's about respecting that health is private. Arq doesn't make customers feel they're doing something illicit. Discretion is a service premium that signals maturity. The brand is openly confident about what it sells — it's the delivery that stays quiet.

06   Visual Identity

What Arq
looks like.

The visual tension in Arq's brand idea — clinical and rebellious — has a direct visual equivalent: the precision of a lab report combined with the restraint of a luxury editorial.


Not clinical white. Not wellness green. Not luxury black-and-gold that reads pharmaceutical gala. Something more honest than all of those. Dark, matte, precise. The kind of packaging you leave on your desk rather than hiding in a drawer.

I
Restraint is the loudest statement
Every competitor shouts. Man Matters uses bright photography and bold headlines. Traya uses warm Ayurvedic tones. Arq uses negative space, precise type, and one gold accent. The restraint communicates confidence. It says: we don't need to convince you.
II
Type does the heavy lifting
No illustrative icons. No lifestyle photography. No before-and-after. A Playfair Display serif — elegant but with edges — and a clean grotesque. The serif carries the editorial intelligence. The grotesque carries the precision. Together they're a biohacker's bedside table: part journal, part lab notebook.
III
One colour rule
Near-black everything. One gold accent — used exactly once per composition, never twice. The gold is not decorative. It marks the thing that matters most on the page. The brand idea. The product name. The price. The call to action. Everything else is dark.
IV
The packaging is the brand
Matte black outer box. No product name visible outside. Gold "arq" stamp on the seal — a wax-seal reference that suggests something premium and private. Inside: clean white inner packaging, product name in Playfair, dosage in DM Mono. The unboxing is the brand experience.

09   Product Naming

What things
are called.

Arq never hides what it sells. The product name is the product name — no euphemisms, no invented brand names for generic drugs. The transparency is the premium. You know exactly what you're getting.


Products are named by their molecule first, descriptor second. The Arq prefix can be used for brand kits: Arq Focus, Arq Perform, Arq Recover. But the molecule name always appears prominently.

Modafinil
arq / focus
Never "smart drug kit" or "cognitive support." It's modafinil. Arq Focus is the optional brand layer for the kit packaging.
Sildenafil
arq / perform
Never "men's wellness" or "performance support." It's sildenafil. No euphemism. The naming respects the customer's intelligence.
Semaglutide
arq / lean
The kit name contextualises without hiding. The molecule name leads everywhere that matters.
THC · CBD Oil
arq / unwind
AYUSH-licensed Vijaya extract. The kit name signals effect without being clinical. The formulation is always stated clearly.
BPC-157
arq / recover
Physician supervised. The molecule name and the kit name work together — one precise, one evocative.
CJC-1295 + Ipamorelin
arq / build
The stack is named plainly. The kit name provides the shorthand without obscuring what's inside.
10   Experience Principles

Every
touchpoint.

Brand isn't a logo or a tagline. It's the sum of every interaction a customer has with Arq — from the first Instagram ad to the unboxing to the refill reminder. Each one must carry the brand idea forward.

📲
The WhatsApp
opening.
First message: "Hey. What are you after?" — not "Welcome to Arq! How can we help you today?" The brand voice in one line. Assumes the customer has already decided.
📞
The doctor
call.
Brisk, specific, respectful. 3–5 minutes for most products. The doctor asks questions, not lectures. Never says "are you sure about this?" — says "here's what you need to know." Ends with: "Your prescription is issued. You'll get a tracking update shortly."
📦
The
unboxing.
Matte black outer. Gold "arq" seal. Plain courier label. Inside: white inner box, product name in Playfair, dosage in DM Mono, one card — the protocol card — with dosing, timing, what to expect. No leaflets. No warnings as cover pages. Clean.
💬
The refill
message.
Day 25: "Your next month's supply — same order?" One tap YES. Ships today. No re-intake. The message doesn't ask how you're feeling. It assumes you know what you're doing, because you do.
🧾
The billing
name.
"Dear One Technologies" on the bank statement. Not "Arq Health" or "Arq Clinic." The holding company name — generic, unmemorable, private. Discretion encoded in the billing infrastructure.
📋
The protocol
card.
Every order includes a single card: product name, dose, timing, one thing to know, one thing to watch. Designed like a well-edited page from a Field Notes notebook. Not a pharmaceutical insert. An informed person's reference.
11   Competitive Landscape

Where Arq
lives.

Every Indian health brand clusters in the same place: functional, accessible, slightly clinical, designed to reduce shame by pretending the condition is normal. None of them are built for someone who is proud of what they're taking.

Man Matters / Mosaic
₹342 Cr revenue. The category leader. Functional, clinical, value-positioned. Their positioning crime: they tell you that your condition is common and nothing to be ashamed of — which paradoxically reminds you that you should be ashamed of it. They treat the customer as a patient managing a problem, not a person with a preference.
Traya
₹236 Cr. Hair loss specialist. Strong at clinical authority and results guarantee. Ayurveda positioning limits their ceiling — they can never own the modafinil or peptide customer. Single vertical brand.
Qurist
The closest thing to Arq's cannabis vertical. AYUSH-licensed, prescription model, home delivery. But functional brand, poor UX, 3-day doctor callback. Premium positioning gap is wide open.
Bold Care
Celebrity-dependent (Ranveer Singh), sexual wellness only, can't expand. No medical rigour — a brand without a clinic. The anti-shame positioning is right but the execution is surface level.
Arq's white space: premium, medically rigorous,
product-first, biohacker-native.
Nobody is there. Nobody is close.

The Hims comparison is useful but not the aspiration. Hims built scale through shame reduction. Arq is built on something more durable: respect. It doesn't tell customers their problem is common. It tells them their knowledge is valid.


That's a different customer. A harder customer to acquire and an impossible customer to lose.
