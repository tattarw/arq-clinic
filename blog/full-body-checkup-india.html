What Packages Actually Test (And Why It's Not Enough)

Standard full body checkup packages are designed for diagnostic efficiency, not prevention. They're fast, cheap to run, and fine for detecting acute illness. They are terrible for catching disease before it starts.

What They Test

  • CBC (Complete Blood Count) Hemoglobin, WBC, platelets — checks for anemia and infection
  • Liver Function Tests (LFTs) ALT, AST, bilirubin — screens for hepatitis or cirrhosis
  • Kidney Function Tests (KFTs) Creatinine, BUN, eGFR — checks if kidneys are failing
  • Lipid Profile Total cholesterol, LDL, HDL, triglycerides — screens for cardiovascular risk
  • Thyroid (TSH only) Tests if thyroid is severely hyper or hypothyroid
  • Fasting Blood Sugar Checks if you're diabetic right now

These are all good tests. For acute illness, they work. A person with hypothyroidism will have abnormal TSH. Someone with diabetes will have elevated fasting glucose. Someone with severe anemia has low hemoglobin. But. acute illness is the endpoint of disease progression. By the time TSH is abnormal, your thyroid has been failing for years. By the time fasting glucose is elevated, insulin resistance has been building for a decade.

What They DON'T Test (And Should)

  • ApoB (Apolipoprotein B) Measures atherogenic particle count — far superior to LDL for heart disease risk. Two people with identical LDL can have vastly different heart disease risk based on ApoB.
  • Lp(a) (Lipoprotein A) Genetic cardiovascular risk factor. Test once, know your lifetime risk. If elevated (>50 mg/dL), your heart disease risk is 2-3x higher. Most people never know this.
  • Fasting Insulin + HOMA-IR Reveals insulin resistance — the precursor to diabetes. Catches metabolic disease 10 years before HbA1c becomes abnormal.
  • hs-CRP (High-Sensitivity C-Reactive Protein) Measures systemic inflammation — independent predictor of heart disease and stroke. Chronically elevated hs-CRP is dangerous, even with normal cholesterol.
  • Homocysteine Amino acid that damages blood vessels and brain. Elevated homocysteine predicts dementia and heart disease. B vitamins lower it. Standard checkups miss it.
  • Free Testosterone (not just total) Total testosterone can be normal while free testosterone (the biologically active form) is depleted. Sexual dysfunction, muscle loss, and fatigue result.
  • Full Thyroid Panel (T3, T4, TPO antibodies) TSH alone misses Hashimoto's, subclinical hypothyroidism, and thyroid autoimmunity. 5% of Indians have thyroid disease undetected by TSH screening alone.
  • Vitamin D (25-OH Vitamin D) 70% of Indians deficient. Causes bone loss, immune dysfunction, and mood disorders. Standard checkups don't measure it.
  • Vitamin B12 47% of Indian vegetarians deficient. Causes anemia, neuropathy, cognitive decline. Shows up on CBC if severe, but subclinical deficiency is common.
  • Ferritin (Iron Storage) Standard checkups check hemoglobin (iron in blood). Ferritin (iron in storage) is depleted first — causing hair loss, fatigue, poor wound healing. A ferritin of 15 is "normal" but functionally depleted.
  • Folate & B vitamins Deficiency causes anemia, mouth ulcers, cognitive issues. Rarely measured outside CBC context.

These aren't exotic tests. They're available in every NABL-accredited lab in India. They just cost more than the package, so they're not included in budget screening.

Why Physician Interpretation Matters (More Than You Think)

Lab reports show each result as "normal," "low," or "high" based on population ranges. Population ranges include sick people. The "normal" range for testosterone, for example, is 300 ng/dL. But a man at 350 is functionally low — he has fatigue, poor recovery, low libido. Technically "normal." Functionally inadequate.

Optimal ranges are narrower. A ferritin of 30 is technically normal, but optimal is 50. A TSH of 2.5 is normal, but optimal for someone with a family history of thyroid disease is <2.0. A fasting insulin of 12 is normal (normal up to 12), but optimal is <5. At 12, you're in early insulin resistance.

A physician who understands prevention interprets your. bloodwork against optimal ranges, not population ranges. They see patterns: elevated fasting insulin + elevated triglycerides + low HDL = metabolic syndrome developing, intervention needed now. They see: elevated hs-CRP + elevated homocysteine + borderline LDL = inflammation-driven cardiovascular risk, not cholesterol-driven, requires different treatment. They see: low Vitamin D + elevated TPO antibodies + normal TSH = early autoimmune thyroid disease, supplementation prevents progression.

A lab report without physician interpretation is raw data. With it, it becomes a roadmap for prevention.

How Often Should Healthy People Test?

The answer depends on baseline results and family history.

  • Completely healthy baseline (all markers optimal, no family history) Every 12 months is reasonable
  • One or more optimal markers in suboptimal range (Vitamin D low, ferritin borderline, TSH on higher end of normal) Annual testing + targeted supplementation/lifestyle changes
  • Family history of early heart disease, diabetes, or autoimmune disease Every 6 months, starting age 30
  • On any chronic medication (blood pressure, thyroid, statin, diabetes) Every 3 months for first 1 years, then annually if stable
  • Multiple metabolic risk factors (obesity, sedentary lifestyle, high stress) Every 3 months to track metabolic changes and response to intervention

  • What's the difference between ApoB and LDL?
    LDL cholesterol measures cholesterol in low-density particles. But particle count matters more than cholesterol amount. ApoB measures the actual number of atherogenic particles, making it superior for heart disease risk. Two people with the same LDL can have vastly different ApoB and therefore different heart risk. For prevention, ApoB is more predictive. Standard checkups measure LDL because it's cheaper — prevention-focused care measures ApoB.
  • What does Lp(a) tell you?
    Lp(a) is a genetically determined risk factor for cardiovascular disease and early heart attack — test once, know your lifetime risk. Unlike LDL (modifiable through diet/exercise), Lp(a) is heritable. If elevated (>50 mg/dL), your heart disease risk is 2-3x higher. Most standard checkups don't measure it — prevention-focused care does. Why? Because if your Lp(a) is high, your prevention strategy changes (potentially earlier statin therapy, closer monitoring). You can't manage what you don't measure.
  • Why is fasting insulin important?

Standard checkups only measure fasting glucose and HbA1c — missing the early warning. Prevention-focused care catches insulin resistance in stage 1, when it's most reversible.
  • What does hs-CRP measure?
    High-sensitivity C-reactive protein (hs-CRP) measures systemic inflammation — a predictor of heart disease, stroke, and metabolic disease. Chronically elevated hs-CRP predicts cardiovascular events independently of cholesterol. Most people with normal cholesterol and elevated hs-CRP still have heart disease risk — but they're not flagged.
