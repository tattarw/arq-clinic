PCOD vs PCOS: What's the Difference & Does It Actually Matter?

April 16, 2026
arq. medical team
Medically reviewed

In India, PCOD and PCOS are used interchangeably — but doctors know there's a difference, and it's worth understanding. Walk into a clinic, mention irregular periods and cysts on your ovaries, and you'll probably hear either diagnosis. But here's what's actually happening: one is a structural condition, and one is a systemic endocrine disorder. The distinction matters less than you'd think — what matters is your metabolic profile. Yet, many women are left confused, thinking the label defines their condition, when really it's the bloodwork that tells the story.

Key takeaways

PCOD vs PCOS in India:

  • PCOD (Polycystic Ovarian Disease): Ovaries release immature eggs → cysts form. Structural. Milder. Affects ~30% of Indian women. Can be managed with lifestyle.
  • PCOS (Polycystic Ovary Syndrome): Endocrine/metabolic disorder. Excess androgens, insulin resistance, irregular periods. Systemic. Requires medical treatment.
  • The Rotterdam criteria require 2 of 3: oligo/anovulation, hyperandrogenism (elevated androgens), polycystic ovaries on ultrasound
  • Ultrasound alone is misleading — 25% of normal women have polycystic-appearing ovaries without disease
  • arq.'s approach: we don't care what you call it. We test your metabolic markers (free testosterone, DHEA-S, fasting insulin, HOMA-IR, LH:FSH, AMH) and treat what's abnormal
  • Even "mild PCOD" with insulin resistance needs intervention

The Technical Distinction: PCOD vs PCOS

In medical literature, the distinction is subtle but real. Let's define it properly.

PCOD: Structural, Not Systemic

PCOD stands for Polycystic Ovarian Disease. The definition is straightforward: your ovaries produce multiple immature eggs (follicles) that don't fully mature or release. Instead, they accumulate on the ovary as fluid-filled sacs, or "cysts." This is the structural finding on ultrasound.

The critical point: structural PCOD doesn't necessarily mean systemic dysfunction. You could have polycystic ovaries and still ovulate regularly, have normal hormone levels, and menstruate every 28-30 days. In that case, you have the appearance of PCOD but not the disease burden. Studies show that 20-25% of women without any reproductive symptoms have ultrasound findings consistent with PCOD. They don't need treatment; their ovaries just look that way.

PCOD symptoms (if present): Irregular or absent periods, difficulty conceiving, mild androgen excess (acne, excess hair growth), weight gain tendency.

PCOS: Systemic Endocrine Disorder

PCOS stands for Polycystic Ovary Syndrome. The name is similar, the condition is categorically different. PCOS is not just polycystic ovaries; it's a metabolic and endocrine disorder characterized by:

  • Hyperandrogenism: Elevated androgens (testosterone, DHEA-S) causing acne, hirsutism (excess hair), male-pattern baldness
  • Insulin resistance: 50-70% of women with PCOS have impaired glucose tolerance or insulin resistance
  • Oligo/anovulation: Irregular or absent ovulation leading to irregular periods, infertility
  • Metabolic dysfunction: Increased risk of type 2 diabetes (30-40% by age 40), cardiovascular disease, metabolic syndrome
  • Polycystic ovaries: As a structural feature, but not the defining one

PCOS is a true syndrome — a constellation of symptoms linked by underlying metabolic dysfunction. It's systemic, not just ovarian.

The Rotterdam Criteria: How Diagnosis Actually Works

Since the late 1990s, doctors have used the Rotterdam criteria to diagnose PCOS. It requires 2 of 3 findings:

  1. Oligo- or anovulation: Irregular or absent ovulation (periods less frequent than every 35 days or absent)
  2. Hyperandrogenism: Clinical (acne, hirsutism, male-pattern alopecia) or biochemical (elevated free testosterone, DHEA-S)
  3. Polycystic ovaries on ultrasound: 12+ follicles per ovary or increased ovarian volume

Here's the problem: this definition makes PCOD and PCOS essentially the same thing in clinical practice. A woman with polycystic ovaries + irregular periods automatically meets criteria for PCOS, even if her androgens are completely normal. A woman with elevated androgens + regular periods but normal-looking ovaries also has PCOS by Rotterdam criteria, even though she has no polycystic appearance.

The distinction in India: Indian physicians traditionally used "PCOD" to describe the structural finding alone (cysts visible on ultrasound). "PCOS" was reserved for the full syndrome with metabolic dysfunction. But in rigorous medical literature, both terms now describe the same Rotterdam-defined condition. The real question isn't PCOD vs PCOS — it's whether the condition carries metabolic complications that require treatment.

Why Ultrasound Alone Is Misleading

This is critical: you cannot diagnose PCOD or PCOS from ultrasound alone. Yet many women hear "you have cysts" and assume they have a disease.

The evidence is clear. Studies show:

  • 20-25% of women without PCOD or PCOS symptoms have polycystic-appearing ovaries on ultrasound
  • These women have normal periods, normal hormones, and no fertility problems
  • Their polycystic appearance is an incidental finding, not a diagnosis
  • Conversely, some women with true PCOS have completely normal-appearing ovaries on ultrasound — they have severe irregular periods and elevated androgens, but ultrasound misses them

A diagnosis of PCOD or PCOS requires more than ultrasound. It requires bloodwork to clarify what's actually happening metabolically.

The Metabolic Workup: What Actually Matters

Here are the tests that determine whether your PCOD/PCOS carries real disease burden and what treatment you need:

1. Free Testosterone & Total Testosterone

Elevated androgens define hyperandrogenism. Free testosterone (not total) is more specific; it reflects bioavailable hormones. If your free testosterone is elevated, that's metabolic dysfunction requiring treatment. If it's normal, you may have structural PCOD but not hormonal PCOS.

2. DHEA-S (Dehydroepiandrosterone Sulfate)

Another androgen marker. Elevated DHEA-S suggests adrenal androgen excess, a different metabolic phenotype than ovarian androgen excess. Some women have adrenal PCOS (elevated DHEA-S) without ovarian manifestations.

3. Fasting Insulin & HOMA-IR (Insulin Resistance Index)

Insulin resistance is central to PCOS pathophysiology. Fasting insulin > 12 mIU/L or HOMA-IR > 2.0 indicates impaired glucose metabolism. This is where most Indian women with PCOS live — insulin resistance, even if they're lean.

Why this matters: Insulin-resistant PCOS requires metformin, inositol, or GLP-1 therapy. Insulin-sensitive PCOS may respond to anti-androgen medications like spironolactone or even lifestyle alone. The metabolic phenotype determines the treatment.

4. LH:FSH Ratio

In PCOS, LH (luteinizing hormone) is often elevated relative to FSH, disrupting the follicle selection process and causing anovulation. An elevated LH:FSH ratio (>2:1 or 3:1) supports PCOS diagnosis but isn't required — some women have PCOS with normal ratios.

5. AMH (Anti-Müllerian Hormone)

AMH reflects ovarian reserve and granulosa cell function. Elevated AMH is common in PCOS and associated with increased ovarian androgen production. AMH > 4.0 ng/mL suggests higher ovarian reserve and confirms PCOS phenotype, but it's not diagnostic alone.

6. Thyroid Function (TSH, Free T4)

Thyroid disease mimics PCOS: irregular periods, weight gain, fatigue. Rule it out. Many Indian women are hypothyroid, compounding metabolic dysfunction.

7. Prolactin

Elevated prolactin causes irregular periods and galactorrhea, mimicking PCOS. A simple prolactin test rules this out.

8. Fasting Glucose & 2-Hour Glucose Tolerance Test (Oral Glucose Tolerance Test)

Many women with PCOS have impaired fasting glucose (100-125 mg/dL) or impaired glucose tolerance. Early detection of prediabetes is crucial for prevention.

The arq. Approach: Metabolic Phenotyping

Here's what matters: arq. doesn't prescribe based on diagnosis. We prescribe based on metabolic phenotype.

You might have:

  • Insulin-resistant PCOS: Elevated fasting insulin, elevated HOMA-IR, often overweight, irregular periods. Treatment: metformin 1500-2000 mg daily, inositol (myo-inositol 2-4g daily), dietary carb control, strength training.
  • Lean PCOS with adrenal androgen excess: Normal insulin, elevated DHEA-S, elevated free testosterone, regular or slightly irregular periods. Treatment: spironolactone or birth control pills, potentially low-dose prednisolone if adrenal contribution is significant.
  • Inflammatory PCOS: Elevated inflammatory markers (high-sensitivity CRP), irregular periods, androgen excess. Treatment: anti-inflammatory diet, omega-3 supplementation, possibly inositol.
  • Post-pill PCOS: Hormone abnormalities that persist after stopping hormonal contraception. Treatment: NAC (N-acetylcysteine), inositol, lifestyle modification.
  • Ovulatory PCOD (benign polycystic appearance): Polycystic-appearing ovaries but regular periods, normal hormones, normal insulin. Treatment: lifestyle optimization, monitoring. Often no medication needed.

The label matters less than the bloodwork. A woman with "PCOD" and severe insulin resistance needs as much treatment as a woman with "PCOS." Conversely, a woman with polycystic ovaries, regular periods, and normal metabolic markers may need no treatment at all.

The Treatment Difference: Type Determines Therapy

Insulin-Resistant PCOS/PCOD:

  • Metformin 1500-2000 mg daily (or extended-release 2000 mg)
  • Myo-inositol 2-4 g daily (powerful synergist with metformin)
  • Low glycemic index diet (whole grains, legumes, non-starchy vegetables)
  • Strength training 3-4x weekly (builds insulin sensitivity)
  • Vitamin D if deficient (very common in India)

Androgen-Excess PCOS/PCOD:

  • Spironolactone 50-200 mg daily (anti-androgen diuretic)
  • Combined hormonal contraceptive (if fertility not desired)
  • Saw palmetto extract (weak anti-androgen, botanical)
  • Low-glycemic diet (still important for hormonal balance)

Lifestyle-First PCOD (benign phenotype):

  • No medication required if ovulating regularly and metabolically normal
  • Regular aerobic exercise 150+ minutes weekly
  • Strength training 2-3x weekly
  • Anti-inflammatory diet (Mediterranean pattern)
  • Annual monitoring of metabolic markers and ovulation

Does PCOD Become PCOS Over Time?

This is a common question from women diagnosed with PCOD in their 20s who wonder if it will "worsen" to PCOS.

The honest answer: not really, but also yes, semantically. Here's why:

If you have structural PCOD now (polycystic ovaries, regular periods, normal hormones), your ovarian appearance won't change. But if metabolic dysfunction develops — insulin resistance from weight gain, androgen excess from aging or other causes, anovulation — then the diagnosis shifts from "benign PCOD" to "metabolic PCOS." It's not the PCOD becoming PCOS; it's the unmasking of metabolic dysfunction that was always present or newly acquired.

The risk factors for progression: sedentary lifestyle, high-glycemic diet, weight gain, age (PCOS manifests more severely in 30s-40s), and genetic predisposition. Regular metabolic monitoring catches early dysfunction before it compounds.

Fertility Implications: When It Matters Most

Here's where PCOD vs PCOS becomes practically critical: fertility.

If you have PCOD with regular ovulation: Fertility is unaffected. Your ovaries release eggs normally. Conception is possible without intervention.

If you have PCOS with anovulation: You don't ovulate consistently. Conception is difficult or impossible without treatment.

The solution is testable and often treatable:

  • Metformin + inositol: Restores ovulation in 40-50% of insulin-resistant women. Combined effect is synergistic.
  • Weight loss (if overweight): Even 5-10% weight loss improves ovulation in 25-30% of women
  • Ovulation induction (if needed): Letrozole or clomiphene citrate to induce ovulation pharmacologically
  • Assisted reproduction: IVF for those who don't conceive with medication + lifestyle

Many women with PCOS conceive naturally after metabolic treatment. The diagnosis is not infertility.

8 Questions About PCOD and PCOS

We've covered the key points above in our FAQ. These questions address the most common concerns and misunderstandings.

Questions

Frequently asked about PCOD & PCOS

  • Is PCOD dangerous?
    PCOD itself — the presence of cysts on ovaries — is not dangerous. Many women without PCOD have cysts; many with PCOD have no problems. What's dangerous is untreated metabolic dysfunction. If your PCOD comes with insulin resistance, elevated androgens, or anovulation (no ovulation), those conditions carry real health risks: infertility, type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease. The cysts are a marker, not the threat. The underlying metabolic dysfunction is. This is why metabolic workup matters more than ultrasound diagnosis.
  • Can PCOD become PCOS?
    Not really. The distinction is more about severity and breadth of dysfunction than a disease progression. If your PCOD is just polycystic-appearing ovaries with regular periods and no hormonal abnormality, it's unlikely to 'become' PCOS unless other metabolic dysfunction develops (insulin resistance, androgen elevation). Conversely, if your PCOD already involves irregular periods and elevated androgens, it's really PCOS with a misleading name. The shift isn't the ovarian appearance changing — it's the diagnosis being reclassified as metabolic dysfunction becomes evident. Regular monitoring and metabolic testing catch this.
  • Which is worse: PCOD or PCOS?
    PCOS is generally more serious because it's a systemic endocrine disorder with metabolic complications. PCOD can be benign (just cysts, no dysfunction). However, a woman with 'mild PCOD' with undetected insulin resistance may fare worse long-term than a woman with diagnosed PCOS receiving metabolic treatment. The name doesn't determine severity — the underlying metabolic dysfunction does. A woman with PCOD and insulin resistance, irregular periods, and elevated androgens (so, really PCOS) needs treatment as urgently as a woman clinically diagnosed with PCOS. The distinction matters less than the metabolic profile.
  • Can you have PCOS without polycystic ovaries?
    Yes. The Rotterdam criteria require 2 of 3: ovulatory dysfunction, hyperandrogenism (elevated androgens), and polycystic ovarian morphology. Many women have PCOS without polycystic-appearing ovaries — they may have perfectly normal-looking ovaries on ultrasound but still have irregular periods, elevated free testosterone, and insulin resistance. This is sometimes called 'lean PCOS' or 'non-polycystic PCOS.' These women don't have the 'classic' ultrasound finding but absolutely need metabolic treatment. Ultrasound diagnosis alone misses them. Bloodwork reveals them.
  • Does PCOD or PCOS affect fertility?
    Only if it involves anovulation (absent or irregular ovulation). If you have polycystic ovaries but ovulate regularly, fertility is unaffected. If you have PCOS with anovulation, you don't release eggs consistently, which impairs conception. The good news: treatable. Metformin improves insulin sensitivity and restores ovulation in 40-50% of women. Combined with inositol, dietary changes, and weight loss (if needed), ovulation often resumes. Many women with PCOS conceive naturally after metabolic treatment. Those who don't have options: ovulation induction (letrozole, clomiphene) or assisted reproduction. The diagnosis isn't destiny.
  • Is ultrasound enough to diagnose PCOD or PCOS?
    No. Ultrasound shows only ovarian morphology. It reveals cysts but nothing about your hormones, insulin, ovulation status, or metabolic dysfunction. Studies show 20-25% of women without any reproductive symptoms have polycystic-appearing ovaries — they don't have PCOS. Conversely, women with true PCOS and no visible cysts are missed by ultrasound alone. Diagnosis requires bloodwork: testosterone, DHEA-S, LH, FSH, fasting insulin, HOMA-IR, thyroid, AMH. Ultrasound is a tool, not a diagnosis. Bloodwork reveals the metabolic reality.
  • Can lifestyle alone treat PCOD or PCOS?
    For some women, yes. Mild PCOD without metabolic dysfunction may resolve with weight loss, exercise, and dietary changes (low-glycemic index, adequate protein, anti-inflammatory). However, many women — even lean women without weight issues — have insulin-resistant PCOS that doesn't respond to lifestyle alone. For them, metformin, inositol, or spironolactone are essential. The determining factor: your metabolic bloodwork. If insulin and androgens are severely elevated, lifestyle alone is insufficient. Is your insulin sensitivity impaired? Are your thyroid and prolactin normal? arq. runs a complete metabolic and hormonal panel and builds your treatment around what's actually abnormal. If you have irregular periods, elevated free testosterone, and insulin resistance, you have metabolic PCOS and need treatment regardless of ultrasound.
