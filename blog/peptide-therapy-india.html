What Are Peptides and How Do They Work?

Peptides are short chains of amino acids — typically 2-50 amino acids long. They're smaller than proteins but large enough to have specific biological activities. Think of them as signaling molecules: they bind to specific receptors on your cells and trigger repair, growth, or optimization responses.

Why peptides are powerful: unlike hormones (which are broadcast signals to your entire body), peptides are targeted. BPC-157 signals your gut lining and tendons specifically. TB-500 targets tissue repair pathways. GHK-Cu activates collagen synthesis. This specificity means fewer off-target side effects than broad-spectrum hormones like testosterone.

How peptides differ from steroids: Steroids (testosterone, nandrolone, oxandrolone) are lipophilic — they cross cell membranes easily and have long systemic effects. They're suppressive to natural hormone production. Peptides are hydrophilic — they work via receptors and are rapidly degraded by proteolytic enzymes. They don't suppress natural hormones. They enhance endogenous signaling.

This makes peptides safer for most biohackers. But it also means they require precise dosing, clean injection technique, and baseline bloodwork to ensure they're working.

Popular Peptides and What the Evidence Shows

1. BPC-157 (Body Protection Compound-157) — The Tissue Healer

What it is: A 15-amino-acid peptide derived from gastric juice, naturally occurring in the body. Originally discovered for gut protection, now used off-label for tendon, ligament, and neurological repair.

What the evidence shows: Animal studies are robust. BPC-157 promotes healing in: tendon tears (Achilles, rotator cuff), ligament damage (ACL, MCL), muscle strains, surgical wounds, and even neurological injuries (stroke, traumatic brain injury). Mechanisms include increased angiogenesis (blood vessel formation), growth factor signaling (VEGF, NGF), and reduced inflammation.

Human evidence: Limited but emerging. A few small studies show efficacy for tendon repair and muscle healing, but we lack large RCTs. Most physician-recommended peptide protocols include BPC-157 for athletes with soft tissue injuries because the risk-benefit is favorable and animal data is strong.

Dosing: 250-500 mcg per injection, typically twice daily, for 8-12 weeks. Injected subcutaneously or intramuscularly near the injury site (for localized injury) or systemically (for gut healing).

Side effects: Minimal. Transient nausea in some users, injection site soreness, rarely increased appetite. No hormonal suppression.

2. TB-500 (Thymosin Beta-4) — The Recovery Amplifier

What it is: A naturally occurring 43-amino-acid peptide found in high concentrations in thymus and wound-healing cells. Endogenous function: cell migration, angiogenesis, and tissue repair.

What the evidence shows: TB-500 accelerates healing in muscle injuries, joint inflammation, and even cardiac tissue damage (heart attack). Studies show it: increases blood vessel formation, promotes cell survival, reduces fibrosis (scarring), and enhances ligament repair. More human data exists for TB-500 than BPC-157, and results are consistent.

Why athletes use it: Faster recovery from training injuries, reduced inflammation, improved joint health. A 400mg course (TB-500 comes in 5-10mg vials) accelerates recovery 3-4 weeks faster than placebo in most users.

Dosing: 5-10 mg per injection, twice per week, for 8-12 weeks. Some protocols use 10mg for 4-6 weeks (higher density, shorter cycle).

Side effects: Very mild. Slight dizziness in some users (one-time, transient), increased appetite, improved sleep quality (often reported as beneficial). No hormonal suppression.

3. GHK-Cu (Copper Peptide) — The Skin Repair Specialist

What it is: A tripeptide (glycine-histidine-lysine) complexed with copper. Naturally occurring, involved in collagen remodeling and skin healing.

What the evidence shows: GHK-Cu increases collagen synthesis, improves skin elasticity, reduces fine lines, and accelerates wound healing. Studies show: enhanced fibroblast activity, increased collagen I and III, and improved dermal density. One of the few anti-aging peptides with solid human data.

Why it's popular: Visible skin improvements. Users report improved texture, firmness, and reduced wrinkle depth within 8-12 weeks. It's also used for hair growth and wound healing.

Dosing: 200-300 mcg subcutaneously daily, or topical application 1-3x daily. Topical is easier, systemic is more potent.

Side effects: Transient nausea (if injected systemically), mild skin dryness (if topical). Very well-tolerated.

4. CJC-1295 / Ipamorelin — The Growth Hormone Secretagogues

What they are: CJC-1295 is a GHRH (growth hormone-releasing hormone) analog. Ipamorelin is a ghrelin mimetic. Both signal your pituitary to release more of your own growth hormone — they don't supply exogenous GH, they amplify endogenous production.

What the evidence shows: Strong effects on GH and IGF-1 elevation. Studies show: increased muscle mass, improved body composition, accelerated recovery, enhanced sleep, and improved collagen production. However, long-term effects on cancer risk or aging are unknown — IGF-1 elevation can be growth-promoting (good for muscle, potentially risky for dormant cancers).

Why athletes use them: Most potent peptides for muscle building and recovery. Often stacked together (CJC-1295 + Ipamorelin) for synergistic growth hormone release. Results include 3-5kg lean muscle gain over 12 weeks with proper training.

Dosing: CJC-1295 100-200 mcg once daily (long half-life), Ipamorelin 100-200 mcg twice daily, or combined in a "blend."

Side effects: Increased appetite (ghrelin effect), potential blood glucose elevation in prediabetic men (IGF-1 increases insulin sensitivity but can dysregulate glucose if baseline is poor), joint pain relief (actually beneficial), improved sleep. More pronounced effects than BPC-157 or TB-500, so monitoring is essential.

5. Selank — The Anxiolytic Peptide

What it is: A synthetic peptide developed in Russia as an anxiolytic (anxiety-reducing) agent. Mechanism: dopamine and serotonin modulation, GABA enhancement.

What the evidence shows: Reduces anxiety symptoms, improves mood, enhances cognitive function. Studies show efficacy comparable to benzodiazepines but without dependency or sedation. Improves attention and memory.

Why it's used: For men with high stress, anxiety, or cognitive fog. Indian professionals under occupational stress report improved clarity and reduced anxiety within 3-4 weeks.

Dosing: 250-500 mcg daily or twice daily, intranasally (spray) or subcutaneous injection.

Side effects: Minimal. Intranasal irritation if inhaled incorrectly, rare headaches. Very well-tolerated.

Legal Status of Peptides in India: The Grey Zone

Peptides are not regulated as drugs in India. They're not explicitly illegal, but they're also not formally approved by the DCGI. This means:

  • Availability: You can buy them from compounding pharmacies without a prescription
  • Risk: Quality control is inconsistent. You don't know if the BPC-157 you're buying is actually BPC-157, or if it's contaminated, or if the concentration is accurate
  • Import: Importing peptides into India for personal use is technically in a legal grey area — not explicitly prohibited, but customs may confiscate them
  • Prescribing: Physicians can prescribe peptides, but only off-label. There's no specific regulatory pathway, so physician discretion and accountability matter

What Bloodwork You Need Before Peptides

Baseline bloodwork prevents harm and establishes whether peptides are working:

Essential baseline (all peptides):

  • Complete metabolic panel: glucose, electrolytes, liver function, kidney function (safety baseline)
  • CBC (complete blood count): check for infections, anemia, or hematologic abnormalities
  • hs-CRP: high-sensitivity C-reactive protein (inflammation marker — peptides should lower this)
  • Liver function tests: ALT, AST, bilirubin (some peptides are hepatically metabolized)
  • Kidney function: creatinine, eGFR, BUN (safety baseline)

Additional if using growth hormone secretagogues (CJC-1295, Ipamorelin):

  • IGF-1: establish baseline and monitor elevation (should rise 20-30% on therapy)
  • Fasting glucose / HbA1c: monitor glucose control (IGF-1 elevation can dysregulate glucose in prediabetic men)
  • Hormonal panel: testosterone, free testosterone, SHBG, prolactin, thyroid (growth hormone secretagogues can shift hormonal balance)

Retest schedule:

  • Week 0 (baseline)
  • Week 6-8 (midpoint check: hs-CRP should be dropping, IGF-1 should be rising if using secretagogues)
  • Week 12 (end of cycle: confirm efficacy, ensure safety markers are normal)
  • After cycle: ensure hormones, inflammation, and organ function return to normal

FAQ: Peptide Therapy

Questions

Frequently asked about peptides

  • Are peptides legal in India?
    Peptides are not classified as drugs under Indian pharmaceutical law — they're considered research chemicals or compounded substances. This means they're not explicitly illegal, but they're also not formally regulated by the DCGI (Directorate General of Pharmaceuticals). They're available from compounding pharmacies without prescription, but this is a regulatory grey area. The absence of regulation is both an opportunity and a risk: you can access them relatively easily, but quality control is inconsistent. arq. only sources peptides from pharmaceutical-grade compounding facilities with quality assurance, not from unregulated suppliers. Some peptides (like some growth hormone secretagogues) may have stricter import controls — your physician confirms legal availability before prescribing.
  • What is the difference between BPC-157 and TB-500?
    Both are tissue-repair peptides, but they work via different mechanisms. BPC-157 (Body Protection Compound-157) is a gastric peptide that promotes tissue healing, reduces inflammation, and has neuroprotective effects. Strong evidence in animal models for tendon repair, gut healing, and tissue regeneration. Evidence in humans emerging but not yet robust. TB-500 (Thymosin Beta-4) is a naturally occurring peptide involved in wound healing and cell migration. Evidence shows it reduces inflammation and promotes tissue repair, especially in muscles and tendons. Both are popular among biohackers and athletes, but BPC-157 has slightly stronger preclinical evidence for structural tissue repair (tendons, ligaments). TB-500 is gentler and more broadly applicable. Many protocols use both together for synergistic effects. Your physician determines which fits your recovery goal.
  • Can peptides replace physical therapy or rest?
    No. Peptides enhance recovery but don't replace mechanical loading, physical therapy, or proper rest. Think of it this way: physical therapy signals your body to rebuild tissue. Peptides accelerate that rebuilding process. But without the signal (therapy and movement), peptides alone are ineffective. A man with a torn rotator cuff who takes BPC-157 but does no shoulder rehab won't heal. A man who takes BPC-157 AND does targeted rotator cuff exercises will heal faster and more completely. Peptides work best as adjuncts to evidence-based rehabilitation. Your physician coordinates peptide therapy with your physical therapist to ensure you're not just chemically forcing healing — you're mechanically reinforcing it. This synergistic approach is most effective.
  • What bloodwork do I need before starting peptides?
    Baseline bloodwork before peptide therapy should include: (1) Complete metabolic panel (glucose, electrolytes, liver function, kidney function) to ensure you're healthy enough for therapy, (2) CBC (complete blood count) to check for infections or blood abnormalities, (3) hs-CRP (high-sensitivity C-reactive protein) to measure baseline inflammation — peptides should lower this, (4) IGF-1 (insulin-like growth factor-1) if using growth hormone secretagogues (CJC-1295, Ipamorelin), (5) Hormonal panel (testosterone, free testosterone, SHBG, prolactin, estradiol, thyroid) to establish baseline if you're taking multiple peptides that may affect hormones, (6) Liver and kidney function tests, as some peptides are hepatically or renally metabolized. Repeat testing at 6-8 weeks and 12 weeks monitors whether inflammation is dropping, hormones are stable, and organ function is preserved. Your arq. physician orders and interprets these, ensuring peptide use remains safe.
  • Are peptides safe long-term?
    Safety data on long-term peptide use in humans is limited — most studies are short-term (12 weeks or less) or animal-based. BPC-157 and TB-500 appear safe in animal studies even at high doses, with minimal side effects reported. However, long-term human data is sparse. What we know: (1) Peptides are degraded by stomach acid and proteolytic enzymes, so they don't accumulate in your body like fat-soluble compounds, (2) They're designed to mimic natural peptides, reducing off-target effects, (3) Immune responses (antibody formation against peptides) are theoretically possible but rare, especially with short-term use, (4) Growth hormone secretagogues (CJC-1295, Ipamorelin) may increase IGF-1 chronically, which has unknown long-term effects on cancer risk or aging. Bottom line: short-term use (8-12 weeks) appears safe based on available data. Long-term use (>6 months continuously) requires ongoing monitoring, periodic bloodwork, and physician oversight. Don't self-prescribe long-term peptides without testing.
  • Can I stack multiple peptides?
    Yes, but only under physician oversight. Popular combinations: BPC-157 + TB-500 (synergistic tissue repair), CJC-1295 + Ipamorelin (synergistic growth hormone release), BPC-157 + GHK-Cu (tissue repair + collagen synthesis). However, stacking increases complexity: more compounds mean more potential interactions, more organs processing them, more biomarkers to monitor. A man on 3 peptides needs more frequent bloodwork (6-weekly at least) than a man on 1 peptide. Your physician must understand pharmacokinetics and interactions. Some peptides compete for transport mechanisms or receptors. Stacking amplifies benefits but also amplifies risks if not carefully managed. Start monotherapy, establish tolerance and benefit, then consider stacking only if justified by your recovery goal and confirmed by bloodwork.
  • What are the side effects of peptides?
    Most peptides have minimal side effects, especially at therapeutic doses. Common minor effects: (1) Injection site reactions — redness, itching, swelling at the injection site (brief, usually resolves in hours), (2) Nausea (especially with GHK-Cu or secretagogues), usually mild and transient, (3) Increased appetite (CJC-1295, Ipamorelin due to ghrelin-mimicking activity), (4) Fatigue or lethargy (rare, usually from hypoglycemia if IGF-1 rises too much), (5) Headaches (rare, usually dehydration-related). Serious side effects are rare at therapeutic doses but possible: (1) Immune reactions to peptides (very rare, manifests as allergic responses), (2) Uncontrolled growth hormone/IGF-1 elevation (acromegaly-like symptoms), (3) Worsening of underlying conditions (e.g., if you have dormant cancer, growth-promoting peptides are risky). Your baseline bloodwork and ongoing monitoring catch problems early. If side effects occur, stopping peptides usually resolves them within days — peptides don't accumulate. This is different from steroid hormones, which have longer half-lives.
  • How long does it take to see results from peptides?
    Results vary by peptide and goal. BPC-157 for tendon repair: 4-8 weeks for noticeable improvement in pain, 8-12 weeks for structural healing (confirmed by imaging if applicable). TB-500 for muscle injury: similar timeline, 6-10 weeks for functional recovery. GHK-Cu for skin: 4-6 weeks for visible improvement in texture and collagen, but full effects take 3 months. CJC-1295/Ipamorelin for body composition: 8-12 weeks for noticeable muscle gain or fat loss, but effects are gradual. Selank for anxiety: 2-4 weeks for mood improvement. Timeline depends on: (1) baseline condition severity, (2) dose consistency, (3) concurrent rehabilitation or lifestyle changes, (4) individual responsiveness (genetic variation). Don't expect dramatic results in 2 weeks. Peptides work via signaling mechanisms, not immediate chemical changes. Patience and compliance matter. Your physician establishes a reassessment timeline (usually 8-12 weeks) to evaluate whether the peptide is working for you. If no progress by 12 weeks, peptide is discontinued and an alternative is considered.
