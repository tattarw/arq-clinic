Propranolol for Performance Anxiety: India's Most. Underused Prescription

April 16, 2026
arq. medical team
Medically reviewed

Your heart is racing. Your hands are trembling. You're sweating before a presentation, interview, or exam. This is performance anxiety — your body's physical response to high-stakes stress. But here's the truth:you don't need to fix your mind to fix your body. Propranolol, a simple beta-blocker, blocks the physical symptoms of anxiety without affecting mental clarity. You can think clearly, remember facts, maintain focus — all while your heart rate and trembling are suppressed. It's not a tranquilizer. It's not sedating. It's the most underused tool in Indian medicine for performance anxiety.

Key takeaways

Propranolol for performance anxiety

  • What it does Blocks adrenaline's effect on heart, causing racing heart, trembling, sweating to stop. Does NOT affect mental clarity.
  • Dosing 10-40mg taken 30 minutes before event. Not daily — as-needed only.
  • Perfect for Presentations, interviews, exams, public speaking, athletic performance. Discrete events with specific physical anxiety symptoms.
  • NOT for Chronic anxiety. Propranolol only blocks physical symptoms, not cognitive worry. For persistent anxiety, SSRIs are first-line.
  • Addiction risk Zero. Non-addictive, no dependence, no withdrawal. Unlike benzodiazepines.
  • Cost in India per tablet. Extremely affordable.
  • Availability Widely available (brands: Inderal, Ciplar, Corbeta). Schedule L — prescription required but easily obtainable.
  • Side effects Low. blood pressure, fatigue, cold hands/feet. Temporary and mild with single-dose use.

What Propranolol Does (And Doesn't Do)

Performance anxiety has two parts:cognitive (worry, fear of failure) and physical (racing heart, trembling, sweating). Most men conflate them. But they're separate systems.

Cognitive anxiety "What if I fail? What if they judge me? What if I forget?" This lives in your prefrontal cortex, the thinking part of your brain.

Physical anxiety Your sympathetic nervous system floods your bloodstream with adrenaline (epinephrine). Adrenaline binds to beta-adrenergic receptors in your heart, blood vessels, and muscles, causing

  • Tachycardia (racing heart, >100 bpm)
  • Trembling (hands, voice)
  • Sweating (palms, forehead)
  • Shortness of breath

Propranolol is a beta-blocker — it prevents adrenaline from binding to these receptors. Result: your heart stays calm, your hands are steady, you don't sweat But your mind remains clear. You can still think, remember, focus, and perform.

This is the critical difference from benzodiazepines (Xanax, Valium), which sedate your entire nervous system — affecting cognition, coordination, and mental clarity. Propranolol leaves your brain untouched.

When Propranolol Works: Performance Anxiety (Events)

Propranolol shines for situational, event-based anxiety where physical symptoms dominate

  • Presentations & public speaking Trembling voice, shaky hands, sweating — propranolol eliminates these. You can think and speak clearly.
  • Job interviews Racing heart, dry mouth, sweating — blocked by propranolol. You focus on answering questions.
  • Exams Physical anxiety can impair exam performance. Propranolol stabilizes you without dulling your mind.
  • Athletic performance Hockey, cricket, tennis, weight training — trembling or racing heart harms performance. Propranolol removes these distractions.
  • Social anxiety (specific situations) Large gatherings, speaking to authority figures, meeting new people — trembling, sweating are suppressed.

Common theme:discrete events with physical symptoms that harm performance. You take propranolol once, 30 minutes before the event, and you're protected for 3 hours.

When Propranolol Does NOT Work: Chronic Anxiety

Do not use propranolol for generalized anxiety disorder (GAD), panic disorder, or persistent worry throughout the day. Here's why

Propranolol only blocks physical symptoms. If you're anxious 8 hours a day, worrying about work, health, finances, relationships, your cognitive anxiety persists. Propranolol doesn't touch it. You'll feel calm physically but still mentally ruminating — a strange, incomplete relief.

For chronic anxiety, you need. SSRIs (sertraline, escitalopram, paroxetine) which address serotonin dysregulation in the brain. SSRIs take 2 weeks to work and are meant for daily use.

The rule Propranolol = event-based, physical anxiety. SSRIs = chronic, cognitive anxiety.

Dosing: How to Use Propranolol

Standard Protocol

  • Dose 10-40mg, most commonly 20mg
  • Timing Take 30 minutes before event
  • Duration Effects last 3 hours
  • Frequency As-needed only, not daily

Most men start with 20mg for their first event and assess response. If symptoms persist, your physician may increase to 40mg for future events. If 20mg leaves you lethargic or too calm, drop to 10mg.

Examples

Presentation starting at 3 PM? Take 20mg at 2:15 PM. Peak effect at 3 PM. You're protected until 6 PM.

Job interview at 10 AM? Take 20mg at 9:15 AM. Peak at 10 AM. Interview protection lasts until 1 PM.

Exam from 2 PM? Take 20mg at 1:15 PM. Peak at 2 PM. Covered for the entire exam.

Baseline Bloodwork & Contraindications

Before starting propranolol, you need basic cardiac and metabolic assessment.

Required Tests

  • Resting blood pressure & heart rate Establish baseline. You're looking for: systolic BP > 100 mmHg, heart rate > 50 bpm.
  • 12-lead EKG Screens for arrhythmias, conduction abnormalities, or other cardiac issues that would contraindicate beta-blockers.
  • Basic metabolic panel Kidney function (propranolol is renally cleared), glucose (propranolol can mask hypoglycemia in diabetics), electrolytes.
  • Thyroid function (TSH, Free T4) Optional but recommended if you have thyroid history.
  • Liver function Hepatic metabolism of propranolol; baseline ensures safety.

Absolute Contraindications (Do NOT Take)

  • Asthma or COPD Beta-blockers constrict airways. Can trigger bronchospasm in asthmatic men.
  • Decompensated heart failure or severe bradycardia (HR < 50 bpm) Propranolol slows heart further — dangerous.
  • Uncontrolled or severe hypertension Paradoxical worsening in some cases.

Cautions (Possible Use With Monitoring)

  • Diabetes (especially insulin-dependent) Propranolol masks hypoglycemia symptoms (trembling, sweating). You might not notice dangerously low blood sugar. Requires glucose monitoring.
  • Low baseline blood pressure (<100/60 mmHg) Propranolol may drop it further, causing dizziness.
  • Thyroid disease Propranolol can affect thyroid function monitoring.

For most healthy men without asthma or cardiac issues, propranolol is safe and well-tolerated.

Side Effects (Temporary With Single-Dose Use)

Propranolol side effects are dose-dependent and usually mild when taken as-needed (single dose).

Common

  • Low blood pressure Systolic BP drops 10 mmHg. You might feel lightheaded or dizzy initially. Usually resolves within an hour.
  • Fatigue or drowsiness Mild and temporary. Most men don't notice it with single-dose use.
  • Cold hands or feet Blood vessels constrict slightly, reducing peripheral circulation. Discomfort is minimal.

Rare (More Common With Daily Use)

  • Bradycardia (slow heart rate, <50 bpm) Usually only occurs with high doses or daily use.
  • Sexual dysfunction Reported with daily use; unlikely with single-dose use.
  • Vivid dreams or sleep disturbances If taken in evening; not an issue with daytime dosing.

Bottom line With single-dose use before events, side effects are minimal and temporary. You'll feel some physical calm, minor dizziness might occur for 5 minutes, then stabilization. By the time your event starts, you're fine.

Propranolol vs Benzodiazepines vs SSRIs

Propranolol (Beta-Blocker)

  • What Blocks adrenaline. Stops physical anxiety symptoms.
  • When As-needed, 30 min before event. Single dose.
  • Mental clarity Fully preserved. You can think and perform normally.
  • Addiction None. Non-addictive.
  • Cost(extremely cheap)
  • Best for Performance/situational anxiety

Benzodiazepines (Alprazolam, Diazepam)

  • What Enhance GABA, sedate central nervous system. Stop physical AND cognitive anxiety.
  • When As-needed or daily, depending on indication.
  • Mental clarity Reduced. Can impair thinking, memory, coordination.
  • Addiction HIGH RISK. Benzodiazepines are habit-forming. Dependence develops quickly. Withdrawal is severe.
  • Cost
  • Best for Acute panic, severe anxiety. NOT for performance anxiety (impairs cognition).

SSRIs (Sertraline, Escitalopram, Paroxetine)

  • What Increase brain serotonin. Address root cause of anxiety.
  • When Daily, long-term (2 weeks to see effect).
  • Mental clarity Normal or improved (as anxiety resolves).
  • Addiction None. Non-addictive.
  • Cost
  • Best for Chronic, generalized anxiety disorder. Panic disorder (combined with CBT).

For performance anxiety Propranolol is superior. No cognitive impairment (unlike benzos), works immediately (unlike SSRIs), no addiction, cheap, available.

arq.'s model isn't propranolol alone. It's propranolol + cognitive-behavioral strategies.

  • Baseline assessment Cardiac and metabolic bloodwork to confirm safety. EKG if indicated.
  • Propranolol dosing Typically 20mg, as-needed 30 min before events. Your physician may adjust based on response.

Questions

Frequently asked about Propranolol

  • How does propranolol reduce anxiety symptoms?
    Propranolol is a beta-blocker that prevents adrenaline (epinephrine) from binding to beta-adrenergic receptors in the heart and nervous system. During anxiety, your body releases adrenaline, causing racing heart, trembling, sweating, and shortness of breath. Propranolol blocks these physical symptoms at their source. Crucially, it does NOT affect the cognitive centers of your brain — your mental clarity and thinking remain unchanged. This makes it ideal for performance anxiety: you can still focus, think clearly, and perform while physical symptoms are suppressed.
  • What is the right dose for performance anxiety?
    Typical dosing: 10-40mg taken 30 minutes before a high-stakes event (presentation, interview, exam). Most men respond well to 20mg. The dose is taken acutely (on-demand), not daily, which makes propranolol unique — you're not on continuous medication. Effects peak at 60 minutes and last 3 hours, making it perfect for single events. Your physician determines your optimal dose based on heart rate, blood pressure, baseline anxiety level, and any contraindications. Start with 10mg for first trial, assess response, then adjust.
  • Is propranolol addictive or habit-forming?
    No. Propranolol is not addictive. It has no potential for abuse, no withdrawal syndrome when stopped, and no dependence risk. Unlike benzodiazepines (alprazolam, diazepam), which are habit-forming and require cautious dosing, propranolol can be used as-needed without concern for addiction. You can take it for one event, then go months without it for the next event, with zero risk of dependence. This is why it's preferred over benzos for performance anxiety — efficacy without addiction risk.
  • Can I use propranolol daily for chronic anxiety?
    Not recommended. Propranolol is approved for hypertension when used daily, but for chronic anxiety, it's suboptimal. The reason: propranolol only blocks physical symptoms, not cognitive anxiety (worrying, catastrophizing, rumination). Chronic anxiety needs SSRIs (sertraline, escitalopram) that address the brain's serotonin system. Propranolol shines for performance anxiety — discrete events with specific physical symptoms. For daily, persistent worry and panic, SSRIs are first-line. If your anxiety is event-specific (presentations, interviews, public speaking), propranolol as-needed is ideal. If it's constant throughout the day, discuss SSRIs with your physician.
  • What are the side effects of propranolol?
    Common (dose-dependent): low blood pressure (dizziness, lightheadedness), fatigue or tiredness, cold extremities (hands/feet feel cold or numb), slowed heart rate (bradycardia), and vivid dreams or sleep disturbances (if taken daily). Rare: sexual dysfunction, shortness of breath in men with asthma/COPD. Most side effects are mild and temporary — they fade within a few hours as the drug wears off. Blood pressure drop is the most common concern, especially if you're already hypotensive. Baseline blood pressure monitoring before starting is essential.
  • Who should NOT take propranolol?
    Absolute contraindications: asthma (propranolol can constrict airways), COPD, uncontrolled heart failure, and severe bradycardia (heart rate below 50 bpm). Cautions: low blood pressure (baseline systolic < 100 mmHg), decompensated heart failure, uncontrolled diabetes (can mask hypoglycemia symptoms), or thyroid disease. If you have any cardiac history, asthma, or significant medical conditions, your physician must evaluate before propranolol. For most healthy men with performance anxiety, it's safe and well-tolerated.
  • What bloodwork is needed before starting propranolol?
    Baseline assessment includes: resting blood pressure and heart rate, basic metabolic panel (glucose, kidney function, electrolytes), liver function tests, and a 12-lead EKG (to rule out cardiac arrhythmias or conduction abnormalities). Thyroid function (TSH, Free T4) is helpful but not mandatory unless you have thyroid history. Fasting glucose is important if you're diabetic, as propranolol can mask hypoglycemia symptoms. Most of these tests are quick and inexpensive. The EKG is critical — it identifies any underlying cardiac issues that would contraindicate propranolol.
  • Can propranolol be combined with other anxiety medications?
    Yes, with caution. Many men use propranolol (as-needed for performance) alongside an SSRI (daily for background anxiety). This combination is safe and effective. The SSRI addresses generalized anxiety and mood; propranolol provides acute symptom relief for specific events. However, combining propranolol with other blood pressure medications or central nervous system depressants (alcohol, sedating antihistamines) requires physician guidance to avoid excessive blood pressure drop or sedation. Never combine propranolol with other beta-blockers or non-selective adrenergic agents without medical supervision.
