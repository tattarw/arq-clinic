The Testosterone Crisis: It's Not Just You

Testosterone levels in men have been declining steadily for decades. Studies show an average drop of 1% per year since the 1980s—a trend that spans continents and affects men of all ages. India is no exception.

A 30-year-old Indian man in 2024 has roughly 25% lower testosterone than a 30-year-old in 1980. This isn't a coincidence, and it's not inevitable.

Why is testosterone falling?

  • Environmental factors Endocrine-disrupting chemicals in plastics, pesticides, and pollution suppress testosterone production
  • Lifestyle collapse Sleep deprivation, chronic stress, and sedentary desk work are testosterone killers
  • Air quality Cities like Delhi, Mumbai, and Bangalore face severe air pollution linked to reduced reproductive hormone production
  • Metabolic dysfunction Processed foods, vegetable oils, and metabolic syndrome sabotage natural testosterone synthesis
  • Over-information, under-action Men know something feels off, but they don't know why or what to do about it
The bottom line Low testosterone in India isn't rare. It's increasingly common. And unlike genetics, it's addressable.

What is Testosterone Replacement Therapy (TRT)?

TRT is straightforward: it's the prescription of exogenous testosterone (externally supplied testosterone) to restore hormonal levels to a healthy, optimal range.

TRT vs. Anabolic Steroids vs. Performance Enhancement

There's confusion here, so let's clarify

  • TRT Medical therapy to normalize testosterone in hypogonadal men (those with clinically low testosterone). Goal: optimal range (200–900 ng/dL). Requires blood work, prescription, monitoring.
  • Performance enhancement Unregulated use of supraphysiological doses (1000+ ng/dL) without medical oversight. No blood work. No doctor. High risk.
  • Anabolic steroids Synthetic hormones designed for muscle gain, not hormone restoration. Often combined with other compounds. Illegal without prescription.

TRT is medical treatment. It's not bodybuilding. It's not cheating. It's correcting a biological deficiency.

Key distinction TRT restores testosterone to normal healthy ranges. It doesn't create superhuman hormone levels. The goal is to feel like yourself again—not to become someone else.

Signs of Low Testosterone

Low testosterone doesn't always announce itself loudly. Often, men attribute symptoms to aging, stress, or depression. Doctors misdiagnose it. Here are the classic signs

Physical Symptoms

  • Chronic fatigue (even after sleep)
  • Loss of muscle mass despite training
  • Increased body fat (especially around the midsection)
  • Difficulty falling asleep or poor sleep quality
  • Low libido or erectile dysfunction
  • Hot flashes or temperature regulation problems

Cognitive & Emotional Symptoms

  • Brain fog and poor concentration
  • Difficulty with memory recall
  • Mood changes (irritability, depression, anxiety)
  • Reduced motivation (at work, gym, life)
  • Social withdrawal
  • Lack of drive or ambition

The Indian Context

In India, these symptoms are often dismissed as "stress" or "modern life." Men may visit a general physician who attributes fatigue to overwork or mood issues to depression—without checking hormone levels. Many never get a testosterone test. Early diagnosis through proper testing can prevent further decline and restore quality of life.

If you recognize yourself You're not weak, lazy, or broken. You may be hypogonadal. That's a diagnosis, not a judgment.

Getting Tested: The Blood Work Foundation

You cannot start TRT without blood work. Period. Testing serves three purposes

  1. Confirm that testosterone is actually low
  2. Identify secondary causes (thyroid dysfunction, pituitary issues, metabolic problems)
  3. Establish a baseline for monitoring

Is TRT Legal in India?

Yes. Testosterone is legal in India when prescribed by a qualified doctor. Here's the regulatory landscape

  • Testosterone is a Schedule H drug in India—meaning it requires a doctor's prescription and can only be dispensed from registered pharmacies
  • No over-the-counter testosterone—unlike in some countries, you cannot legally buy testosterone without a prescription in India
  • Any qualified doctor can prescribe it—endocrinologists, urologists, general practitioners, or specialists in sexual health
  • Monitoring is expected—legal TRT involves regular blood tests and clinical follow-ups, not one-time prescriptions

The challenge isn't legality—it's access. Many general practitioners in India don't regularly prescribe TRT because they lack training or confidence in dose adjustment. Patients often have to seek out specialists.

TRT Formulations Available in India

India has several testosterone formulations. Here's what's available and what works

Oral Testosterone (Least Preferred)

  • Testosterone Undecanoate (Andriol, Restasis) Oral, taken with food. Dose: 40–160 mg/day. Pro: Easy to take. Con: Inconsistent absorption, requires multiple daily doses, can stress the liver.
  • Methyltestosterone Rarely used anymore due to hepatotoxicity and poor efficacy.

Injectable Testosterone (Gold Standard)

  • Testosterone Enanthate Intramuscular injection, once weekly. Dose: 50–100 mg/week. This is the standard choice worldwide and in India. per vial.
  • Testosterone Propionate Shorter-acting, requires more frequent injections (every 2–3 days). Rarely used for TRT.
  • Testosterone Cypionate Similar to enanthate. Less commonly available in India.

Transdermal (Patches/Gels)

  • Testosterone Gel (Testogel, Androgel) Applied daily to skin. Dose: 50–100 mg/day. Pro: Non-invasive, steady levels. Con: Expensive in India, can transfer to partners/children, less convenient.
  • Testosterone Patches Rarely available in India. Expensive.
Most common in India Testosterone Enanthate injections (intramuscular, weekly) because it's effective, affordable, and widely available.

TRT Dosing & Monitoring Protocol

Standard Dosing

  • Starting dose 50–100 mg/week intramuscularly (test enanthate)
  • Titration Dose adjusted every 4–6 weeks based on symptoms and blood work
  • Maintenance dose Typically 75–200 mg/week (varies by individual)
  • Goal Achieve total testosterone 600–900 ng/dL and free testosterone 15–25 pg/mL

Side Effects & Monitoring

TRT is generally safe when monitored, but side effects can occur

Common Side Effects (1–10%)

  • Acne Usually mild and manageable with skincare
  • Fluid retention More common at higher doses
  • Increased aggression or irritability Dose-dependent; usually resolves with dose adjustment
  • Libido changes Initially may increase, then can paradoxically decrease if estradiol gets too high

Serious Side Effects (Rare, <1%)

  • Polycythemia Increased red blood cell production. Monitored via hemoglobin. Managed by donating blood if needed, or dose reduction.
  • Sleep apnea exacerbation TRT can worsen pre-existing sleep apnea. Screening recommended before starting.
  • Gynecomastia Breast tissue growth if estradiol rises too high. Managed with aromatase inhibitors or dose adjustment.
  • Infertility TRT suppresses LH and FSH, which can suppress sperm production. Reversible upon stopping or with hCG co-administration.
  • Cardiovascular risk Controversial. Meta-analyses show mixed results. Men with pre-existing heart disease should discuss TRT risk-benefit with a cardiologist.

Why monitoring matters Most side effects are dose-dependent and preventable through regular blood work and dose adjustment. This is why DIY TRT (without a doctor) is dangerous—you have no way to catch rising hemoglobin, high estradiol, or liver enzyme elevation.

Special Scenarios

TRT & Fertility

TRT suppresses sperm production by suppressing LH and FSH. If you want to preserve fertility while on TRT, options include

  • hCG (human chorionic gonadotropin) Co-administered alongside testosterone. Dose: 500–1,000 IU 2–3 times/week. Maintains testicular function and sperm production.
  • Clomiphene citrate Stimulates natural testosterone production without exogenous testosterone. Better option if you want to maintain natural fertility.
  • Sperm banking If fertility is critical, bank sperm before starting TRT.

TRT & Pre-Existing Medical Conditions

  • Prostate cancer TRT is contraindicated. History of prostate cancer = do not start TRT.
  • Untreated sleep apnea Screen and treat sleep apnea first; then start TRT cautiously.
  • Severe cardiovascular disease Discuss risk-benefit with your cardiologist. TRT may be contraindicated.
  • Liver disease Oral testosterone is risky; injectables safer. Monitor liver function closely.
  • Diabetes TRT may improve insulin sensitivity; compatible with careful monitoring.

Why DIY TRT is Dangerous

Many men in India buy testosterone from underground markets or without medical supervision. This is high-risk

  • Unregulated products No quality assurance. You don't know what you're injecting.
  • No dosing guidance Men often use supraphysiological doses (500+ mg/week) that cause serious side effects.
  • No baseline testing You don't know your starting testosterone or other hormone levels.
  • No monitoring No blood work to check for polycythemia, high estradiol, liver damage, or cardiovascular strain.
  • No medical guidance If side effects occur, no doctor to help manage them.
  • Legal risk Buying testosterone without a prescription is illegal. Police occasionally crack down on steroid dealers.

Lifestyle Optimization (Before & Alongside TRT)

Not all low testosterone requires medical TRT. Many men improve with lifestyle changes

Sleep

  • Testosterone is produced during deep sleep (REM and slow-wave sleep)
  • 7–9 hours/night is essential. Sleep deprivation kills testosterone production.
  • Fix sleep apnea, sleep consistency, and sleep environment before considering TRT

Strength Training

  • Resistance training raises testosterone temporarily and chronically
  • Focus on compound movements (squats, deadlifts, bench press, rows)
  • 3–4 sessions/week is optimal

Nutrition

  • Adequate protein (0.8–1g per lb bodyweight)
  • Adequate fat (especially saturated fat and cholesterol—testosterone is made from cholesterol)
  • Micronutrients: Zinc, magnesium, vitamin D (deficiency = low testosterone)
  • Minimize processed foods, excess seed oils, and sugar

Stress & Recovery

  • Chronic stress elevates cortisol, which suppresses testosterone
  • Meditation, yoga, or breathwork helpful
  • Adequate rest days between training

Weight Management

  • Obesity (especially belly fat) increases aromatization—testosterone converts to estradiol
  • Weight loss alone can raise testosterone 15–25%

Important Lifestyle changes take 8–12 weeks to show results. If you're severely symptomatic (non-functional fatigue, inability to work/exercise), don't wait. Start TRT while optimizing lifestyle.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is TRT legal in India?

Yes. Testosterone is a Schedule H drug requiring a prescription. Any qualified doctor can prescribe it legally. What's illegal is selling testosterone without a license or buying it without a prescription.

Can I get TRT without blood work?

No. Blood work is essential—not optional. It confirms hypogonadism, rules out secondary causes (thyroid, pituitary issues), and establishes baseline health markers. Without blood work, you're guessing and risking side effects.

Does TRT cause infertility?

TRT suppresses sperm production (LH and FSH drop). If you want to preserve fertility, discuss hCG co-administration with your doctor. hCG maintains testicular function and sperm production while you're on TRT. Once you stop TRT, fertility usually returns within 6–12 months.

What are the risks of TRT?

Common: acne, fluid retention, mood changes (usually dose-dependent). Rare but serious: polycythemia (high red blood cells), sleep apnea worsening, gynecomastia (breast tissue growth if estradiol is high). Very rare: cardiovascular issues. Regular monitoring catches all of these early.

Why is DIY TRT dangerous?

DIY TRT involves unregulated products (you don't know what you're injecting), no baseline testing, no dosing guidance, no monitoring, and no medical support if side effects occur. You risk liver damage, cardiovascular strain, fertility loss, and serious hormonal imbalances.
