Questions

Frequently asked about Padel

Why do I cramp during padel?
Padel cramps during competition are typically triggered by electrolyte depletion—specifically magnesium and sodium. Explosive court movement burns through intramuscular magnesium 3x faster than steady endurance exercise. Dehydration concentrates electrolytes in sweat, depleting tissue reserves. Testing RBC magnesium (the cellular measure, not serum) reveals subclinical deficiency even when standard tests appear normal. A physician can assess your individual sweat rate and repletion strategy.
What blood tests should padel players get?
Core markers for padel athletes include magnesium (RBC), testosterone (energy, power, recovery), ferritin (oxygen delivery and work capacity), hs-CRP (inflammation and injury risk), Vitamin D (joint health, bone density), and cortisol (overtraining signal). Most players also benefit from complete metabolic panel, iron studies, and thyroid screening. Testing frequency depends on your level—competitive players benefit from quarterly monitoring during the season to track recovery and adjust protocols.
How can I prevent padel shoulder injuries?
Padel shoulder injury prevention involves three pillars: structural (deload timing, rotator cuff conditioning), inflammatory (tracking hs-CRP, omega-3 status), and recovery (testosterone, cortisol balance, sleep). High hs-CRP before symptoms predict injury 6-8 weeks ahead. Low Vitamin D accelerates rotator cuff pathology. Biomarker monitoring lets your physician optimize your deload cycle, identify when inflammatory load is too high, and coordinate strength programming with hormonal recovery capacity.
Is magnesium supplementation enough for court sports?
Magnesium supplementation without testing is guesswork. First, establish your baseline RBC magnesium and whether you're truly depleted. If levels are low, supplementation works—but timing, form, and dosing matter. Glycinate absorbs better than oxide. Some athletes need electrolyte repletion during play, not just supplementation. Your physician can build a padel-specific protocol: in-match sodium-potassium strategy, post-match repletion timing, and seasonal dosing adjustments. Test, don't guess.
What supplements actually work for padel players?
Effective supplements are biomarker-driven. Low magnesium? Glycinate + taurate. Low ferritin? Iron + Vitamin C. High hs-CRP? Curcumin, omega-3 (test your Omega-3 index first), berries. Low testosterone or elevated cortisol? Ashwagandha, zinc, adaptogenic mushrooms. Low Vitamin D? High-dose D3 based on baseline. Supplement companies sell universally—your physician prescribes specifically. Most padel players improve performance 15-40% on targeted protocols versus generic supplement stacks, because the recommendations are based on actual deficiencies.
Is padel safe for players over 40?
Padel is safe for 40+ players, but the margin for error shrinks. Testosterone naturally declines, recovery capacity decreases, bone density (especially women) needs monitoring, and cardiovascular stress is higher. Age 40+ requires proactive biomarker screening: testosterone, cortisol, inflammatory markers, bone health (Vitamin D, calcium, magnesium), and cardiac risk factors. Many 40+ padel players play at high intensity safely because they monitor these markers and adjust training volume accordingly. Without this monitoring, risk of tendon injury, fracture, and overuse syndrome increases significantly.
How does padel recovery relate to testosterone and ferritin?
Testosterone and ferritin are core recovery drivers. Testosterone regulates protein synthesis, red blood cell production, and motivation—low levels slow adaptation. Ferritin carries oxygen; low ferritin means poor aerobic recovery and fatigue. A padel player with testosterone 450 and ferritin 25 will feel perpetually tired even with adequate sleep. One with testosterone 650 and ferritin 70 rebounds faster between matches. Testing these markers lets your physician optimize training load. If ferritin is low, aggressive iron repletion can cut recovery time 20-30%. Protocol-driven hormone support works when guided by actual data.
